# Abstract 

Modern audio is created by mixing stems from different sources, raising the question: can we independently watermark each stem and recover all watermarks after separation? We study a separation-first, multi-stream watermarking framework –embedding distinct information into stems using unique keys but a shared structure, mixing, separating, and decoding from each output. A naive pipeline (robust watermarking + off-the-shelf separation) yields poor bit recovery, showing robustness to generic distortions does not ensure robustness to separation artifacts. To enable this, we jointly train the watermark system and the separator in an end-to-end manner, encouraging the sep-arator to preserve watermark cues while adapting embedding to separation-specific distortions. Experiments on speech+music and vocal+accompaniment mixtures show substantial gains in post-separation recovery while maintaining perceptual quality. 

Index Terms : audio watermarking, source separation, multi-stream, joint training 

# 1. Introduction 

Recent advances in AI-generated speech [1, 2, 3, 4] and neu-ral audio editing have made it increasingly difficult to protect the copyright and provenance of digital audio content. Audio watermarking has therefore emerged as a key technology for copyright protection and tamper detection [5]. Classical audio watermarking methods usually operate directly in the time or frequency domain, such as phase coding, spread-spectrum em-bedding, and low-bit coding [6]. To improve robustness and payload capacity, deep neural audio watermarking methods have been proposed, including WavMark [7], MaskMark [8], and SilentCipher [9]. These ap-proaches demonstrate strong robustness–imperceptibility trade-offs, enabling higher payloads while maintaining perceptual au-dio quality. However, modern generative pipelines often combine au-dio from multiple sources and rights holders. A single syn-thetic waveform may contain a watermarked music accompa-niment and a separately watermarked or unwatermarked vo-cal stream. Existing watermarking systems are typically de-signed for single-watermark, single-carrier settings and degrade sharply in such multi-source mixtures. A natural strategy is to apply a music source separator (e.g., Demucs [10], TF-GridNet [11], Open-Unmix [12], or Spleeter [13]) to decompose the mixture into stems and then detect wa-termarks per stem. In practice, separation is not lossless: even

2.2.1. Encoder. 

Given an n-second audio segment and a binary secret key, the encoder produces a bounded watermark perturbation in the time–frequency domain. The audio is transformed into a mag-nitude spectrogram, while the key is embedded into a condi-tioning vector. A stack of Conformer [15] blocks, equipped with Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) layers [17] im-mediately after the LayerNorm of each of its four core mod-ules (two FFNs, MHSA, and CONV), predicts a real-valued modification mask applied to the magnitude spectrogram. The modified spectrogram is then inverted to the time domain us-ing the original phase to produce the watermarked waveform. The modified spectrogram is inverted to wavedomain combined with the original phase to produce the watermarked waveform. 

2.2.2. Decoder. 

The decoder takes a potentially distorted audio segment and pre-dicts the embedded bits. It follows a RobustDNN-style [18] ar-chitecture operating directly on the waveform, using multiple 1D convolutional layers to extract watermark-related features before bit prediction. 

2.3. Source Separation Network 

2.3.1. Demucs 

We employ Demucs [10] as our source separation network. De-mucs operates directly in the time domain, which is crucial for our framework as it avoids potential artifacts introduced by time-frequency transformations that could misalign or dis-tort the embedded watermark signals. Its U-Net-like encoder-decoder architecture with strided convolutions enables multi-scale feature extraction while maintaining fine temporal details through skip connections [19]. The bidirectional LSTM [20] layers at the bottleneck effectively model long-range dependen-cies in audio, which is essential for coherently separating over-lapping sources and preserving the continuity of watermark pat-terns. We use the standard Demucs architecture without modifi-cation to leverage its proven separation performance and ensure our findings are not biased by architectural changes. 

2.4. Impact of Separation on Watermarking 

Source separation is inherently lossy [21]. Audio separators often introduce artifacts such as phase misalignment, spectral smearing [22], and cross-source interference (e.g., residual vo-cals in the separated accompaniment track)-limitations explic-itly acknowledged by the authors of Demucs themselves [14]. These artifacts act as non-stationary, structured noise that is fun-damentally different from the generic distortions (e.g., noise addition, compression) against which most watermarking sys-tems are robust. Consequently, a watermark decoder trained only for generic robustness fails to decode correctly from sepa-rated stems, as shown in our experiments (Section 4). This mis-match necessitates a co-design approach where the watermark-ing system learns to be robust to the specific distortion profile introduced by the separator, and the separator is encouraged to preserve features critical for watermark recovery. 

2.5. Joint Training Pipeline 

To bridge the robustness gap identified in Section 2.4, we pro-pose an end-to-end joint training framework that optimizes the watermarking encoder/decoder and the Demucs separator con-currently. This co-optimization is the core of our contribution. It allows the gradient from the watermark decoding loss to flow back through the separator, guiding it to preserve watermark-relevant features in its separated outputs. Simultaneously, the watermarking encoder learns to embed information in a man-ner that is more resilient to the specific, non-linear distortions introduced by the separation process. For each stem (e.g., vocal and accompaniment), we sample a full-length audio clip (approximately 20 seconds). From each clip, a 2-second segment is cropped to serve as the car-rier for watermarking. The 2-second duration represents a standard trade-off in audio watermarking, providing suffi-cient temporal context ( L = 88200 samples at 44 .1 kHz) for encoding a payload of 32 bits while maintaining com-putational efficiency for AURA* . 2.6. Datasets and Training Setup. 

The watermarking model is pre-trained on approximately 150 hours of instrumental music from Free Music Archive (FMA) [23] and 300 hours of speech from the Emilia [24] dataset. This pre-training phase is conducted on four NVIDIA RTX 4090D GPUs for 48 hours. Additional training uses MUSDB18 [25] dataset. The end-to-end joint training is per-formed on four NVIDIA L20 GPUs for 72 hours. All audio is resampled to 44.1 kHz, and two-second segments are used as watermark carriers. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10 −4 and initialize from pre-trained checkpoints.

2.8. Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate audio fidelity using Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and Scale-Invariant Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SI-SNR) to quan-tify waveform reconstruction (accounting for gain fluctuations), and ViSQOL [28] for human-perceived quality on a 1–5 Mean Opinion Score (MOS) scale.

Separation integrity. A critical question is whether opti-mizing for watermark robustness degrades the core functional-ity of the Demucs separator. Table 3 addresses this by compar-ing separation quality before and after joint training using stan-dard source separation metrics (SDR and SI-SDR). These negligible differences confirm that our framework enhances wa-termark robustness without compromising the separator’s abil-ity to produce high-quality stems. In other words, the joint training preserves the original performance of the separation network while adding watermarking capabilities. 

2.10. Robustness against separation 

To evaluate whether watermarking methods can preserve de-coding accuracy after separation, we adopt a separation-first robustness standard aligned with the training pipeline in Sec-tion 2. Watermarks are embedded independently into two-second segments from different sources (speech+music and vo-cal+accompaniment), then mixed and separated using Demucs. BER is computed by decoding the aligned segments from each separated stem. As shown in Table 1, conventional neural wa-termarking methods such as WavMark and Audioseal exhibit unacceptable bit recovery after separation, with BER deterio-rating to 20-30% on both benchmarks. AURA* provides only a minor improvement, suggesting that robustness to average at-tacks alone is insufficient to guarantee robustness to source sep-aration. In contrast, the jointly optimized models generalize sub-stantially better to the 32-bit multi-stream separation set-ting. Both joint and finetuned variants achieve BER of ap-proximately 1% on the speech+music benchmark, while the fine-tuned model further improves performance on the vo-cal+accompaniment benchmark (1 % BER). This improvement indicates that incorporating genuine vocal and accompaniment data enables the model to resolve the severe harmonic entan-glement and spectral overlap that are inherently absent in arti-ficially spliced speech and music mixtures. These results im-ply that end-to-end coupling enables the separator to preserve watermark-relevant cues and encourages the encoder–decoder to adapt to separation-specific artifacts. Consequently, the in-terference caused by multiple watermarks embedded across dif-ferent stems can be reduced to an acceptable level for reliable decoding. 

# 3. Conclusion 

We studied the feasibility of separation-first multi-stream audio watermarking and showed that robustness to generic distortions does not guarantee robustness to source separation. By jointly training the watermarking system and the separator end-to-end, we substantially improve post-separation decoding accuracy while maintaining competitive perceptual quality. These results suggest that separation-aware training is essential for reliable watermarking in modern multi-source generative audio pipelines. While our current framework is limited to two-stem mixtures, future endeavors will aim to expand this joint optimization to more complex, multi-stem scenarios. 4. Generative AI Use Disclosure
